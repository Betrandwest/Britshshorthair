The British Shorthair is a medium to large cat breed with a short, dense coat and a distinctive round head and face, much like a cuddly teddy bear. British Shorthair cats are sweet and affectionate without being petty or clingy. The breed originated in England where it naturally evolved and was the original short-haired domestic cat of the region. Originally, many British Shorthairs were dyed blue (a shade of gray), but today's British Shorthairs are made in a veritable rainbow of colors and patterns, including solids, partial colors, brindle, tortoiseshell, and calico.

History Of The British Shorthair Breed

The British Shorthair is probably the oldest cat breed in Britain. The most widely held theory of the breed's evolution is that the British Shorthair began as a short-haired street cat, but breeders refined and standardized the cat to become the breed we know and love today. It is said that it was made to resemble a British Shorthair. The British Shorthair was one of the breeds exhibited at the first cat show held in England in 1871. At that time, the breed was called the British Blue. This is due to the fact that blue (grey) was a very common color in this breed, especially early on. Blue British Shorthairs are still popular, but come in many different colors and patterns. The British Shorthair was first brought to America in the early 1900s and was called the House Shorthair. The British Shorthair was officially recognized by the Cat Lovers Association in 1980. Today this popular breed can be found all over the world. British Shorthair Kittens For Sale

British Shorthair Cat Care

As the breed name suggests, the British Shorthair has a short coat and requires very little grooming. A British Shorthair should be brushed once a week by him with a soft, straight brush. Brush more often during shedding season to remove shedding before it spreads throughout your home. Clip nails every 2 weeks and check inside the ears weekly. If you see a little dirt in the ears, use pet ear cleaner and cotton balls to clean the ears with cotton balls (never use cotton swabs). If your British Shorthair's ears are red or excessively dirty, consult your veterinarian. British Shorthairs are playful into adulthood, but not hyperactive or overactive. They get plenty of exercise by exploring the house and playing with you several times a day. Keep your British Shorthair busy with a quill, toy mouse and other favorite toys. Make sure there are plenty of things for your cat to climb and perch on, such as scratching poles, cat-friendly bookshelves, and cat apartments.
